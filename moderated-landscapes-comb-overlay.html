Capability — What this means for Canva

Reduce what "capable" needs to mean

6 of 7 participants could search and use templates effortlessly, but struggled with AI prompting. Only P7 found prompting natural. The gap isn't awareness — it's the effort of translating intent into a text prompt. Provide pre-built prompts, contextual suggestions, and "refine this" buttons that require zero writing skill.

P1: "It's way easier for me to just find the template and then add the colors myself than it is to prompt." | P6: "I tried to format it into the slide with bullet points. And it cannot do that."
Opportunity — What this means for Canva

Fill the manual valley & own the pre-Canva phase

The middle 30% of every workflow has zero AI presence — users default to manual not by choice but because AI doesn't surface there. Meanwhile, 3 of 7 participants start in ChatGPT or Gemini before even opening Canva. Two interventions: (1) lightweight contextual AI assists during manual work, and (2) an AI-powered planning step at project creation to capture the ideation phase.

P2: "In the middle, assuming I can do most things on my own without AI… I use it less." — not "don't need" but "AI doesn't offer." | P6: "I need you to make this more fun… put a spin on this" — said to ChatGPT, not Canva.
Motivation — What this means for Canva

Reframe AI as the easier path, not the more powerful one

Users who resist AI aren't unaware of its power — they perceive it as more effort than the manual alternative. P4 avoids AI across 70% of her workflow. P3 only generates when search fails. P5 wants "human touch." Target moments where AI is genuinely less work than manual, and make that comparison obvious in the UI.

P4: "It's almost like going with what you know and going with the path of least resistance." — Make AI be the path of least resistance. | P3: "I don't like using AI if I don't have to because of the sustainability issues."

Easy — What this means for Canva

Collapse search-to-generate into one action

P3's workflow: search → find nothing → switch to generate. That's a 3-step decision. When search results are weak, show "Generate this instead" pre-populated with the search query. One click, zero prompting. For P7 prompting is already effortless — study what makes it natural for him and replicate that simplicity for others.

P3: "There's no options that have anything like what I'd be looking for. So that's where I'd go to generate." — Make that transition instant. | P1: "It's way easier for me to just find the template and then add the colors myself than it is to prompt."
Attractive — What this means for Canva

Show, don't ask — preview AI outputs before prompting

Instead of a blank prompt field, show previews of what AI could generate based on the current design context. P2 valued creative outputs beyond the library. P6 found Magic Media attractive when it worked. But P4 found AI output quality uncertain and unattractive. Reduce uncertainty by showing before asking.

P2: "If there's something really creative or gimmicky… I'll probably try and have AI specifically generate it." | P4: "I would use AI as like a last resort" — uncertain quality makes it unattractive.
Social — What this means for Canva

Introduce social proof to normalise AI usage

Completely absent. No participant referenced what others created with AI. Zero social proof in the current experience. Surface community AI creations, show usage stats ("12K presentations used this AI feature this month"), and blend AI-generated templates alongside human-created ones. P4's "path of least resistance" mindset would soften if she saw peers using AI successfully.

No participant quoted peer behaviour or community examples. Social is the only EAST dimension with zero coverage across all 7 workflows — making it potentially the highest-leverage intervention.
Timely — What this means for Canva

Two timing interventions: before the editor and during the valley

Before: Catch users at project creation with "What are you making? Let AI help you plan." Claims the ChatGPT/Gemini phase. During: Surface micro-assists in the manual middle — layout suggestions, colour harmonisation — when users are in flow but fatigued. AI works at workflow edges but is absent during the manual valley.

P6 uses ChatGPT for ideation before Canva. P2's valley is manual by default, not by choice. P1's AI usage only starts at step 4 of 6 — a timing miss for the first 55% of her workflow.

System 1 — What this means for Canva

Blend AI results into the existing search flow

Search, template selection, manual customisation, and library browsing are all System 1 for every participant. They feel like flow. When P1 searches templates and scrolls, an AI-generated option appearing in that same grid — not in a separate tab, not behind a prompt field — would be System 1 compatible. The user doesn't have to decide to "use AI." It just appears. P7 is the north star: AI is his System 1. He's done it enough that prompting is automatic.

P1: "The best thing about Canva is the templates… there's a lot of variability." — AI should add to that variability, invisibly. | P7: "I directly jump and use AI because with a detailed prompt I can explain exactly what I want… without wasting time."
System 2 — What this means for Canva

Eliminate the blank prompt field

Every AI interaction (except P7's) requires System 2 activation — slow, deliberate, effortful. A blank text field is peak System 2: it demands users formulate a thought before receiving anything. Replace with contextual suggestions, visual prompt builders, or "make this more vibrant / professional / playful" one-click options that keep users in flow. The moment you ask them to open a side panel and type, you've forced System 2 — and that's where 6 of 7 participants resist.

P6: "Something off-putting… is when I need to describe this in more detail." — The prompt field is the friction point. | P2: "In the middle, assuming I can do most things on my own without AI… I use it less." — Not by choice, but by design.
